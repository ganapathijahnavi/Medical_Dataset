# Albumin

**Normal Range (Reference Intervals):**  
- Adults: **3.4 – 5.4 g/dL**  
- Children: **3.8 – 5.4 g/dL**  
- Newborns/infants: **3.5 – 5.4 g/dL**  


**Clinical Significance:**  
Albumin is the most abundant protein in the blood, produced by the liver. It helps maintain **oncotic pressure** (fluid balance between blood and tissues) and serves as a carrier protein for hormones, drugs, and electrolytes.  

- **Low albumin (hypoalbuminemia):** Suggests liver dysfunction, kidney disease, malnutrition, or systemic inflammation.  
- **High albumin (hyperalbuminemia):** Rare, usually due to dehydration.  


**Future Diseases Associated with Abnormal Levels:**  
- **Liver cirrhosis** (decreased synthesis)  
- **Nephrotic syndrome** (protein loss in urine)  
- **Severe malnutrition / protein-energy wasting**  
- **Chronic infections or inflammation**  
- **Heart failure** (fluid overload leading to low albumin levels)  
- **Dehydration** (falsely high albumin)  


**Lifestyle Recommendations:**  
- Eat a balanced diet with adequate protein (lean meat, fish, legumes, eggs, dairy).  
- Avoid excessive alcohol (to protect liver).  
- Manage kidney and liver conditions with medical follow-up.  
- Stay well-hydrated.  
- Regular monitoring if at risk (chronic disease, elderly, hospitalized patients).
